Grouting Design for Winnipeg’s Glacial Lake Sediments

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

SEE MORE →

The subsoil profile changes more than you would expect from one end of Winnipeg to the other. In River Heights, you might hit stiff, overconsolidated glacial till within a few meters, while just east across the Red River in St. Boniface, the stratigraphy often shifts to thick sequences of soft, compressible Lake Agassiz clays interbedded with silt lenses. Designing a grouting program here requires moving beyond generic injection parameters and tailoring the method to the specific lacustrine deposit you are working with. A permeation grout that works nicely in the silty till of Charleswood will simply disappear into the high-plasticity varved clays of Transcona unless the rheology and injection pressure are adjusted. We regularly combine the initial grouting design with a soil grain size analysis to determine the groutability ratio before selecting the binder type and stabilizing additive.

In Winnipeg’s varved clays, grouting success depends on matching the binder’s particle size to the soil’s smallest pore throat, not just the average permeability.

Process overview

Winnipeg sits at an elevation of roughly 239 meters above sea level, a topographic subtlety that hides the depth of the former glacial Lake Agassiz basin—a depression filled with up to 20 meters of soft, high-moisture clays overlying limestone bedrock. This lacustrine origin creates a geotechnical environment where hydraulic conductivity can vary from 10⁻⁶ cm/s in a sandy silt seam to less than 10⁻⁸ cm/s in the intact clay matrix. Effective grouting design in this context involves selecting a binder that can penetrate the targeted seam without hydrofracturing the surrounding clay structure. For projects near the Assiniboine River, where artesian conditions are occasionally encountered, we often integrate the grouting plan with in-situ permeability testing to calibrate the injection pressures and verify that the grout takes hold in the intended strata rather than migrating into the riverbank. The typical approach involves staged injection using microfine cements or colloidal silica, monitored with real-time pressure and volume loggers to avoid surface heave in adjacent roadways like Portage Avenue.

Local context

An observation we make repeatedly on Winnipeg job sites is that the varved structure of Lake Agassiz clays creates a hidden risk during injection: the summer silt layers are far more receptive to grout than the winter clay layers, leading to preferential flow paths that bypass the target zone entirely. If the design does not account for this anisotropy, you end up with a grout curtain that looks complete on paper but has ungrouted clay lenses that later become seepage paths, especially during spring thaw when the Red River rises and groundwater levels spike. The most serious failures we have investigated involved hydrofracture of the clay matrix from excessive injection pressure, which opened cracks that later expanded during freeze-thaw cycles, actually increasing the bulk permeability of the formation instead of reducing it. A conservative, stage-up injection sequence with a low-viscosity starter grout, followed by a higher-yield mix, tends to produce a more homogeneous treated zone.

Additional services

01

Permeation Grouting Design

For silty sand seams and granular lenses within the Lake Agassiz sequence, we design low-pressure permeation programs using microfine cements or colloidal silica to reduce hydraulic conductivity and control groundwater inflow during excavations.

02

Compaction and Compensation Grouting

Where existing foundations have settled due to clay consolidation or where underground infrastructure is being installed beneath sensitive structures, we design controlled compaction grouting plans with real-time heave monitoring to lift and relevel without overstressing the soil.

03

Jet Grouting for Cutoff Walls

For deep excavations near the Red or Assiniboine rivers, we specify jet grouting parameters to create overlapping soilcrete columns that serve as temporary or permanent groundwater barriers, designed to withstand the hydrostatic pressure at the limestone interface.

Top questions

What is the typical cost range for a grouting design and supervision package in Winnipeg?

For a standard project such as a building foundation cutoff or a utility trench seal, the design, specification, and field supervision package generally ranges from CA$1.540 to CA$6.320, depending on the number of injection points, depth of treatment, and whether laboratory groutability testing is required beforehand.

How do you determine whether the soil is groutable before starting field work?

We perform a grain-size distribution test (sieve and hydrometer) to calculate the groutability ratio, comparing the soil’s D15 with the proposed grout’s D85. For Winnipeg’s silts and clays, this ratio often falls between 5 and 15, which indicates that permeation is possible but requires careful pressure control, or that a different technique like jet grouting should be considered.

What grout types work best in Winnipeg’s high-plasticity clays?

High-plasticity Lake Agassiz clays are generally not permeation-groutable because of their small pore throats. In these formations, we typically shift to compaction grouting with a stiff, low-slump mortar to densify the surrounding soil, or to jet grouting if a cutoff wall is required. The choice depends on the project objective: seepage control versus strength improvement.

How do you prevent grout from migrating into the river during waterfront projects?

We design the injection sequence to start farthest from the riverbank and move toward it, using a lower-pressure, higher-viscosity grout at the riverward end. Real-time monitoring of injection pressure and volume, combined with dye tracing in observation wells, confirms that the grout stays within the intended treatment zone.

Can grouting stabilize the limestone bedrock interface where overburden has eroded?

Yes, we often encounter voids and softened zones at the clay-limestone contact in Winnipeg. For these conditions, we design a two-phase program: first, a low-viscosity microfine cement to fill the small fissures in the bedrock surface, followed by a higher-yield grout to plug any larger voids, creating a stable bearing layer for deep foundations or tunnel construction.
