Raft and Mat Foundation Design in Winnipeg’s Heavy Clays

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

SEE MORE →

Winnipeg sits on one of the most challenging foundation soils in Canada: the glaciolacustrine clays of former Lake Agassiz. These high-plasticity clays can swell when wet and shrink during drought, exerting tremendous stress on conventional footings. Over decades of local practice, the engineering community here has learned that a rigid raft or mat foundation is often the most reliable way to float a structure over this active zone. We apply the mat foundations approach when the bearing stratum is deep and differential movement must be controlled across the entire building footprint. In neighborhoods like River Heights or along the Red River floodplain, where seasonal moisture swings are extreme, a well-designed raft distributes loads broadly and reduces the risk of partition-wall cracking that plagues isolated pad foundations.

A properly designed raft foundation turns Winnipeg’s expansive clay problem into a uniform support system, eliminating the differential movement that cracks walls and jams doors.

Process overview

The National Building Code of Canada (NBCC) requires that foundation systems on sensitive clays account for both bearing capacity and total and differential settlement under service loads. In Winnipeg, that means we design mat foundations to CSA A23.3 with reinforcement patterns that resist the curvature induced by edge-lift and center-lift heave. Our process always begins with a detailed geotechnical profile: Atterberg limits, moisture content versus depth, and undrained shear strength from field vane tests or triaxial compression. We model the slab-soil interaction using modulus of subgrade reaction values calibrated to local till and clay units. Because frost penetration here reaches 2.4 meters, we integrate rigid insulation below the mat perimeter and sometimes combine the design with stone columns where soft, compressible silts are found at depth. The result is a monolithic mat that moves as a unit rather than breaking at construction joints.

Local context

The freeze-thaw cycle in southern Manitoba creates a risk that shallow foundations simply cannot tolerate. When saturated clay freezes, ice lenses form and lift the soil by several centimeters; when it thaws, the soil loses strength and the structure settles unevenly. A raft foundation mitigates this by bridging local soft spots, but it is not immune to poor preparation. The most common failure we see is inadequate drainage: if water ponds against the mat edge, the clay beneath swells asymmetrically and the slab tilts. That is why our designs always include a perimeter drain at the footing level, sloped to a positive outfall or sump. We also specify a minimum 150 mm compacted granular base to break capillarity and provide a working platform that stays stable during spring breakup.

Additional services

01

Geotechnical investigation for mat foundations

Rotary sonic or hollow-stem auger drilling to recover undisturbed Shelby tube samples of the Lake Agassiz clay, with laboratory testing for strength, consolidation, and swell potential.

02

Structural design and finite-element modeling

We model the raft as a plate on elastic springs, using subgrade reaction profiles matched to the site stratigraphy, and detail reinforcement for flexure, shear, and punching at column locations.

03

Construction-phase QA/QC and instrumentation

Compaction testing of the subbase, concrete cylinder breaks, and installation of settlement monuments or heave pins to verify that the mat performs within the predicted movement envelope.

Top questions

What is the typical cost range for a raft foundation design in Winnipeg?

For a single-family home or small commercial building, the engineering design fee usually falls between CA$1,640 and CA$5,790, depending on the complexity of the soil profile, the number of column loads, and the level of construction review required. Larger industrial mats are quoted on a project-specific basis.

Why are raft foundations recommended more often in Winnipeg than in other Canadian cities?

The extensive deposits of high-plasticity Lake Agassiz clay make differential movement a primary concern. A raft bridges non-uniform soils and reduces the risk of angular distortion that damages finishes, whereas isolated footings in these clays often require deep piles to reach competent till.

Does a mat foundation eliminate the need for piles in Winnipeg?

Not always. Where the clay is very soft or the structural loads are extremely high, we may design a piled raft that shares load between the mat and deep foundations. The decision depends on settlement tolerances and the depth to the glacial till, which can be 15 to 25 meters below grade in parts of the city.

How do you protect a raft foundation from frost heave in Winnipeg?

We place rigid extruded-polystyrene insulation (typically Type IV) below the mat and extending horizontally beyond the building line. The insulation keeps the subgrade temperature above freezing, preventing ice-lens growth even during sustained -30°C winter periods.
